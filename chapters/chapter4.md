## üìñ Topic of the Day:  Phrases

A phrase is a small group of words that work together as a unit but do NOT have both a subject and a verb.  

It gives extra meaning in a sentence but cannot stand alone as a complete sentence.

Phrases and Idioms look similar because both are groups of words‚Äîbut they are not the same.

| Feature           | Phrase          | Idiom           |
|-------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Meaning           | Literal         | Non-literal     |
| Hidden meaning    | ‚ùå No           | ‚úÖ Yes          |
| Can modify?       | Yes             | No              |
| Easy to understand? | Yes           | No              |
| Example           | ‚Äúon the table‚Äù  | ‚Äúhit the sack‚Äù (go to sleep) |

‚úÖ When a **verb + preposition** (or verb + adverb) come together and act as one meaning, they form a **phrasal verb**.  
‚úÖ The meaning is different from the individual words.  
‚úÖ You cannot understand phrasal verbs by translating words individually.  

- Look (see) + after (behind) ‚â† seeing behind  
- **Look after** = take care of  

So, verb + preposition act as one unit with one new meaning.

‚úÖ **Formula**  
**Verb + Preposition = Phrasal Verb (new meaning)**

### Simple Example
**Verb:** look ‚Üí meaning: to see  
**Verb + Preposition:** look after ‚Üí meaning: to take care of (not literally ‚Äúsee after‚Äù)

**Example sentence:**  
- I look after my little sister.  
Here, **look + after** act as one unit with a new meaning ‚Äî not just seeing something.

## Mind the Topic üí°

üëâIdioms are expressions with meanings you cannot guess from words directly.  
üëâThey do **not** follow a grammar formula like phrasal verbs.

Because they do **NOT** fit the formula:  
**Verb + Preposition/Adverb = New meaning**

| Idiom                  | Why not phrasal verb?                  |
|------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| Break the ice          | Has noun, not a preposition            |
| Spill the beans        | No verb+prep formula                   |
| Under the weather      | Not verb+prep, and meaning is figurative |
| Round the clock        | No verb at all                         |
| Burn the midnight oil  | Longer phrase, hidden meaning          |

**üß†Final Rule**

**Phrasal Verb**  
- Follows a grammar formula  
- Verb + preposition / adverb  
- Meaning may be partly guessable  
- Example: ‚Äúlook after‚Äù, ‚Äúbreak down‚Äù, ‚Äúget up‚Äù

**Idiom**  
- Based on figurative / hidden meaning  
- No fixed structure  
- Meaning is not obvious from the words  
- Example: ‚Äúunder the weather‚Äù, ‚Äúbreak the ice‚Äù, ‚Äúspill the beans‚Äù
